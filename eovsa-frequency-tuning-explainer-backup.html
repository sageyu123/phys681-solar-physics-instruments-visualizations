Three Frequency Conversions, One Tuned RF Slice

This page turns the EOVSA downconversion chain into a visual story. Adjust the RF tuning slot, then watch the same spectral slice move through the tunable first mixer, the fixed second LO, and the digitizer aliasing step.

Stage 1: νLO1 = νRF,low + 20.5 GHz

The key idea is not just that frequencies move. The ordering flips each time a mirror conversion happens. After one mirror the RF order is inverted, after two it is restored, and after the third it is inverted again by the 1200 MHz digitizer clock.

0. Mixer Math

A mixer multiplies two sinusoids: RF at frequency fRF and local oscillator at fLO. The product contains no energy at the original frequencies — only at their sum fLO + fRF and difference |fLO − fRF|. A bandpass filter then selects whichever product is useful. The same colormap (turbo_r) colors the RF band in Stage 1.

1. Tunable first conversion
The RF band is mirrored around a selectable first LO and lands in the fixed high-IF window.

RF to high IF

For a chosen RF slice, EOVSA sets the first LO so that the selected band maps into the fixed high-IF filter. Because this is a mirror conversion, the low RF edge becomes the high edge after mixing.

2. Fixed second conversion
A fixed 21.15 GHz LO mirrors the high IF down to the low-IF band.

High IF to low IF

The second mirror undoes the first inversion, restoring the original RF spectral order. The IF filter selects the useful band inside the broader 600–1200 MHz passband.

3. Digitizer as a third conversion
Sampling mirrors the second IF into baseband; the details depend on the digitizer clock.

Low IF to digitized IF

With the 1200 MHz production clock, the second IF lands cleanly in the target band. This is a third mirror, so the final digital ordering is inverted relative to the original RF slice.
