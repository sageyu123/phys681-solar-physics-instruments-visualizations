PHYS 681 · BREMSSTRAHLUNG
From one close encounter to the free-free continuum

An electron flying past an ion is pulled off its straight line for a fleeting moment; that acceleration radiates. Section A visualizes a single encounter. Section B integrates many of them over a Maxwellian population to build the thermal spectrum.

Section A · One encounter
A single electron deflects past an ion and radiates
Smaller b, slower v, or higher Z makes the bend stronger. The strongest acceleration, and therefore the brightest burst, occurs near closest approach.
The ion sits at the origin. The electron arrives from the left at impact parameter b. The shaded annulus has area 2πb db — the geometric element students integrate over to get the cross section. The rose two-lobed fan marks the nonrelativistic dipole pattern, strongest perpendicular to the instantaneous acceleration toward the ion. Section B · Thermal ensemble
Thermal bremsstrahlung in the Dulk-style thick/thin view
At low radio frequency the source is thick, so Tb saturates near Te and Sν rises like ν2. At high frequency it becomes thin, so Tb falls and Sν flattens.
Left panel: Tb(ν) for a uniform thermal free-free source. The low-frequency plateau is the optically thick limit Tb ≈ Te; above the turnover, Tb drops as the optical depth falls below unity.
Tb or Sν curve
νref
ντ≈1
Dulk-style radio model used. The radio optical depth is approximated as τν ∝ Z2EMT-3/2ν-2gff,radio. We then plot Tb = Te(1 - e-τν) and a fixed-area flux proxy Sν ∝ ν2Tb. That is the thermal-bremsstrahlung shape in Dulk 1985: low-frequency ν2 rise in flux, a nearly flat high-frequency tail, and a brightness temperature that saturates at Te when thick.
