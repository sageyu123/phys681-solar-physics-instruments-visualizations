1. Split levels and allowed transitions

Each level with total angular momentum J splits into 2J + 1 magnetic sublevels. A photon can connect only states with Δm = -1, 0, +1.

2. Spectrum built from those transitions

The wavelength shift of each transition depends on the difference of magnetic energy shifts between the upper and lower sublevels. Transitions landing at the same Δλ add into one stronger observed component.

5. Reading guide

The component labels follow the common selection-rule notation: σ+ for Δm = +1, π for Δm = 0, and σ− for Δm = -1.

In the wavelength convention used on this page, σ+ is the blue-shifted component and σ− is the red-shifted component. Some books flip this sign labeling depending on whether they emphasize photon angular momentum or wavelength shift, so the important thing is to track the stated Δm convention.

The spectrum panel includes the viewing angle θ between the line of sight and the magnetic field, plus an azimuth angle φ for the linear-polarization reference frame. For θ = 0° only the circularly polarized σ components are seen and V is strongest. For θ = 90° the circular signal vanishes, the π component appears, and the linear-polarization signal rotates between Q and U as φ changes.

The MO mixing slider is a toy magneto-optical control. It does not solve the full Unno-Rachkovsky transfer problem; it simply mixes Q and U and adds a small dispersive distortion to V so you can see qualitatively how propagation effects can couple the Stokes parameters.

The relative line strengths are computed from the angular-momentum coupling factor S ∝ (2Ju + 1) | (Ju 1 Jl; -mu q ml) |2, so the anomalous examples naturally produce uneven line amplitudes and more than three components.

The horizontal axis is shown in arbitrary Lorentz-like wavelength units, so the page emphasizes the pattern formation rather than a specific dimensional calibration.
