Why the first amplifier matters

The dominant idea is simple: a warm connector adds its own noise term εT1 and also multiplies every amplifier term by (1 + ε), while the second-stage amplifier is divided by the first-stage gain G2 before it is referred back to the input.

Input-referred receiver noise temperature

Trx = εT1 + (1 + ε)T2 + (1 + ε)T3/G2 + ...

Sign convention: 0 ≤ ε < 1 is the fractional power loss between the feed and the first amplifier, and gains are linear multiplicative factors.

What To Notice

The page is designed so the main conceptual mapping clicks before the student reads every caption.

1. The first amplifier usually owns the budget.

With the lecture numbers, the first amplifier is responsible for nearly all of Trx.

2. Front-end loss is doubly painful.

It adds its own warm term and also inflates every later amplifier term by (1 + ε).

3. Gains in front shield you from later noise.

The second-stage amplifier may be hot physically, but its input-referred contribution is divided by G2.

4. Later stages matter even less.

Because the lecture chain also has G3 = 100, a hypothetical fourth stage would be divided by G2G3 before it reaches the input reference plane.

Teaching objective: within 10 seconds, students should see that loss before the low-noise amplifier hurts every downstream term, while a strong first-stage gain suppresses later electronics when noise is referred back to the input.
